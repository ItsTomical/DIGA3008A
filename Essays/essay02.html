Essay 2

The internet, geopolitics, and digital coloniality

13 June 2025

A critical exploration of how state power, colonial histories, and platform governance intersect online. This essay uses Pakistan’s 2024–25 X (Twitter) ban as a case study to examine digital coloniality, censorship, and the unequal control of internet infrastructure in the Global South.

The internet isn’t just a tool; it’s a space where power and politics collide, especially for countries that are still dealing with the legacy of colonialism as they try to shape their own digital futures. Although often seen as liberating, digital technologies often deepen existing colonial structures by influencing how knowledge, authority, and resources are controlled and shared globally (Birhane, 2020).

In February 2024, during Pakistan’s highly contested general election, the government shut down mobile internet and blocked access to X (formerly Twitter) after a viral video exposed alleged vote tampering (Hussain, 2024). NetBlocks tweeted on 17 February 2024: “Confirmed: Live metrics show a new national-scale disruption to X/Twitter in #Pakistan amid escalating unrest and protests over allegations of election fraud, following a high-level resignation and public admission of vote manipulation by a senior election official” (NetBlocks, 2024).

Pakistan’s actions reveal how governments assert control over digital space while remaining dependent on Western-owned platforms, an ongoing tension at the heart of digital colonialism. This reflects what Ballatore, Graham, and Sen (2017, p. 1211) call digital hegemony, where few countries shape how information flows and who gets to control it. Using Pakistan’s X ban as a case study, this essay explores how geopolitics and digital coloniality intersect, and how both state power and global tech giants shape online control.

As late as January 2025, Pakistan’s ban on X remained in place, with the Pakistan Telecommunication Authority whitelisting thousands of VPNs to regulate internet usage (Khilji, 2025). This ongoing restriction highlights the tension between the government’s efforts to control online spaces and the reality that Western tech companies own and manage much of the internet’s infrastructure and platforms. In other words, Pakistan operates within a digital space it neither fully owns nor controls. While the government asserted its authority by blocking access to X, it had to do so within systems controlled by foreign powers. As Graham (2014) reminds us, the internet is not a placeless space but one deeply shaped by geographies.

Shutting down X wasn’t just about technical control; it was a clear show of power by the Pakistani government; a way to control the narrative both at home and abroad. In April 2024, Reuters reported that the Ministry of Interior defended the ban by saying X ignored government orders and didn’t do enough to stop people misusing the platform. In court, they said the move was necessary “in the interest of upholding national security, maintaining public order, and preserving the integrity of our nation” (Shahzad, 2024). The ministry also claimed that X wasn’t cooperating to fix the problem. On the other hand, X said it was still working with the government to address their concerns (Shahzad, 2024).

Meanwhile, Alp Toker, director of the internet monitoring group NetBlocks, said “Pakistan’s election day internet blackout follows several months of nation-scale network censorship targeting former Prime Minister Imran Khan and his party, PTI” (Civicus Monitor, 2024). The shutdown wasn’t just a one-off; it was part of a longer campaign to control what people see and say online. It also showed how governments aren’t just focused on what’s happening at home, but on managing how they’re perceived around the world.

Platforms like X play contradictory roles depending on who controls them. For activists and everyday people, X is a space to speak up, organise, and take part in democracy. But for governments, it can be seen as a threat or source of international embarrassment, turning it into an instrument of suppression. In Pakistan, viral hashtags and live updates gave people a way to highlight problems with the election, but that same visibility made X a target for censorship. As Mohamed et al. (2020) point out, digital technologies aren’t neutral; they tend to reflect and reinforce the power dynamics already in place, including geopolitical power and colonial legacies.

Many Western-based platforms often do not fully meet content removal demands from governments abroad, leading some countries to threaten or enforce bans on material they consider unlawful or threatening. Gillespie (2018, p.36) explains this tension well, pointing out that most major social media platforms are legally based in the United States, where laws give them strong protections. However, these same platforms also serve users in countries with stricter rules and legal expectations, especially when it comes to responding to government or court requests to take down content (Gillespie, 2018, p.36). Because platforms like X mostly follow rules set in Western countries, they often miss or ignore local needs and political situations in other parts of the world. This can lead to a clash of values: X sees itself as a champion of free speech, while governments like Pakistan’s say restrictions are necessary to protect national security, and sometimes accuse the platforms of being biased or interfering. These tensions are part of a bigger conversation about who gets to make the rules for the internet. Pakistan’s ban on X highlights some of these issues and shows how complex digital power and control really are.

Pakistan’s ban also exposed a deep digital divide; not just in who can get online, but who gets to speak and be heard. Initially, users with VPNs could bypass the block, continuing to post updates and reach global audiences. But many others were cut off completely. This uneven access shows digital inequality is about power, voice, and the chance to participate in public conversations, not just infrastructure. By August 2024, the Pakistan Telecommunication Authority had whitelisted over 20 000 VPNs, mostly for business u5ers, effectively deciding who could bypass censorship and who couldn’t (Khilji, 2024). Digital rights activist Usama Khilji tweeted, “Most popular VPNs appear to be blocked in Pakistan, with variance across varied ISPs. Signifies state & @PTAofficialpk efforts to solidify censorship & move Pakistan towards the Chinese model of firewall, despite liberal constitutional rights. Higher judiciary must step in” (Khilji, 2024). This isn’t just censorship; it’s about shaping the digital space in a way that benefits some and excludes others. As Robinson et al. (2015, p. 570) point out, digital inequality isn’t just about technology; it’s about whether people have agency and participation online.

Pakistan’s X ban is not an isolated incident. Similar platform controls during protests have also occurred in countries like India, showing that fights over who controls online platforms are playing out all over the world. And it’s not just about Western tech giants dominating the Global South. It’s also about how governments try to manage these platforms, sometimes relying on them and sometimes pushing back. In Pakistan’s case, the ban was clearly aimed at silencing critics, but it also showed something deeper: the government doesn’t have much control over the platforms it depends on. That’s the bigger issue here. Digital power isn’t shared equally, and many countries are stuck in a tough position of trying to use platforms that are shaped by others, while also pushing back against the influence they carry.

In conclusion, Pakistan’s ban shows how state power, colonial history, and big tech all collide in today’s digital world. Shutting down X wasn’t just about blocking a platform, it was a political move to silence critics and protect the image of a fragile election. But it also revealed something deeper: how little control countries like Pakistan actually have when the digital infrastructure they depend on is owned and run by Western companies. This is what digital coloniality looks like in real time: a postcolonial state caught between pushing back and still needing the systems it’s trying to resist.
