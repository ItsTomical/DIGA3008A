Week 5 - Design Section

This week, I am further defining and justifying my design decisions based on Blog 4. Since my wireframes and user flow are already structured, I am focusing on refining my fonts, colour choices, and layout to improve both usability and visual appeal.

Refining my Design Choices

Navigation and User Flow

My website’s navigation remains structured around a sticky navigation bar. This will ensure that employers and collaborators can quickly access my portfolio; peers and lecturers can easily find my blog posts and essays; and casual viewers can explore my portfolio highlights and general updates.

In Blog 4, my user flow diagram defined key interactions. I am keeping this structure but will consider fine-tuning button placements, menu behaviour, and scroll interactions – like when you are on the home page and click on the sticky navigation bar to go to the blogs, it scrolls down the home page to that section.

Font Update: Georgia vs Tinos

While refining my font choices, I discovered that Georgia isn’t a free font, which could cause accessibility issues. To keep the same classic and professional look, I chose Tinos, a similar open-source font.

Here’s an image comparison of Georgia vs Tinos to show how this change affects the visual hierarchy and readability of my site.

Colour Palette Refinement

My new palette comprises the following colours:

  • White (background)
  • Almond (soft neutral for warmth)
  • Ash Gray (subtle grey for contrast)
  • Cambridge Blue (muted blue-green for accents)
  • Jasper (rich red for highlights)
  • Delft Blue (deep blue for emphasis)

My original palette idea included:

  • White
  • Almond
  • Timberwolf
  • Cadet Gray
  • Cambridge Blue
  • RISD Blue

Why the change? My original palette had softer, low-contrast colours, making it harder to highlight key elements. Replacing Timberwolf and Cadet Grey with Jasper (a richer red) and Delft Blue (a deeper blue) improved contrast and readability, making headings, buttons, hyperlinks and interactive elements stand out more clearly.

Layout and Composition

Building on Blog 4’s wireframes, and taking inspiration from some of my reference websites, I am continuing to refine the grid system to create a clean, balanced, and visually appealing layout. Focus will be on using white space for improved readability; ensuring that buttons and interactive elements are prominent; and aligning sections for a strong visual flow.

Conclusion: This week, I focused on refining my colour choices, fonts, and layout to improve contrast, readability, and usability. My updated colour palette and font I think keeps my website visually cohesive and easy to read.

Week 6 - Design Section

This week, I am further finalising my design decisions for the website, including my colour palette, fonts, layout, and wireframes.

Style Guide:

Goal & Aesthetic

My goal and aesthetic for my website remain the same, where I aim to showcase my portfolio in a simple yet engaging manner, making it easy for potential employers, collaborators, peers, and casual viewers to explore my work. The aesthetic is clean, modern, and minimalistic, ensuring the content is accessible and easy to navigate.

Visual Design Elements
Colour Palette Refinement

I refined my colour palette and decided on my primary, secondary, and neutral colours. This refinement ensures a cohesive and well-balanced visual identity for my website.

The primary colours provide strong contrast and readability; the neutral colours create a clean foundation; and the secondary colours add visual interest and highlight key elements. I included black in the palette.

Primary Colours:
  • Black (#000000) – Strong contrast colour for text, icons, and key elements.
  • Delft Blue (#3C415B) – A deep, refined colour for the main background, headings, buttons, and key UI components.
Neutral Colours:
  • White (#FFFFFF) – Used for big headers to ensure clean contrast and readability.
  • Almond (#E6DACE) – A warm, soft neutral for block/card backgrounds and subtle section dividers.
Secondary Colours (Accents):
  • Ash Grey (#B2C7B6) – A muted green, good for highlights and soft interactive elements.
  • Cambridge Blue (#8DA69B) – Might also be used for card backgrounds or supporting elements like borders and hover effects.
  • Jasper (#B75B47) – A warm, contrasting colour used for numbering, emphasis, CTAs, or other supporting elements.
Font Choices and Updates

Fonts will be used as follows:

  • Headings and Main Text: Poppins – A modern, sans-serif font for headings, subheadings, and other key text elements. It gives the website a clean, contemporary look.
  • Body Text: Tinos – A serif font chosen for its readability in longer passages, ensuring clarity and comfort when reading detailed content.
Navigation & Interface Elements

I’ve kept my navigation elements mostly the same and added some new additions for better navigation.

  • Buttons: Used for actions like “Read More”, “View”, and “Back to Top”.
  • List Boxes: On the Blogs page, users can select a post from a list, and the page will scroll to it.
  • Sticky Navigation Bar: On the Home page, clicking a section (e.g., Portfolio) will smoothly scroll to it. On other pages (Blog, Essay, Design Process, Portfolio), it will navigate to the respective page.
  • Icons: Quick visual links for Contact info and GitHub.
  • Breadcrumbs (New Addition): Helps users understand where they are and easily navigate back.
Accessibility Considerations

As part of my website’s accessibility improvements, I’ve made changes to ensure that all images have alternative text descriptions and that the navigation bar is keyboard accessible.

Feedback and Iteration

I’ve received feedback from a few friends and peers regarding the design and they mentioned that the button colours could be more prominent. I will adjust the hover effects and CTAs accordingly in the coming weeks.

Reference Material

  • The Seth Hampton portfolio website is my main inspiration. It can be found here. I like the layout of the site. Initially, I wanted my website to look like this, all on one page. However, in the end I found it to be a little much. What I don’t like about it is when you’re on a section such as “about” or “resume”, you have to scroll through the entire section to be able to proceed to the next section of the webpage.

  • I was also inspired by the Maya Nelson layout on Wix, which can be found here. I like the layout and design of the information in this example. It almost appears to be in the form of postcards that align in the centre of the page. What I don’t like, however, is the colour scheme. It’s a bit too bland. Initially, I thought it might be a somewhat soothing colour, but I want viewers to be energised and excited by my work, so I needed some colour that would be more active.
