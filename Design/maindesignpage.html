Design Process

Curious about the work behind my website? Explore my design process – where creativity meets strategy, and pixels find their purpose. See how it all comes together!

My Goal

My goal for this website is to create a platform that showcases my portfolio in a simple, engaging, and accessible way. I want to attract a diverse audience, including potential employers, collaborators, peers, and casual visitors like friends and family by presenting my work through a clean, modern, and minimalistic design that prioritizes ease of navigation and visual clarity.

What I want users to see and do

I want users of my website to easily view and explore my work, giving insight into my creative process and providing an overview of my background and skill set. I want users to explore my portfolio and projects, and to read my blog posts and essays. The aim is to spark their interest in what I have to offer and for them to act by reaching out to me for feedback, employment or collaboration.

Goal Alignment

My User

My target audience for my website includes the different types of visitors listed below, all of which will have different needs:

  1. Potential employers and collaborators: Evaluate my skills and quality of work for potential work opportunities.
  2. Peers and lecturers: View my work for feedback, interest, and academic purposes.
  3. Casual viewers: Friends or family, visiting out of interest and for an engaging experience.

My Content

The content of my website includes:

  1. Portfolio component: Visual displays of my works/projects, with descriptions.
  2. Blogs and essays: Reflections on design, interaction, and media.
  3. Design component: A look at how I design and develop this website.
  4. About and contact component: Biography about my background, skills, and contact information.

The most important and interesting content includes:

  • The most important content is what best represents my capabilities.
  • For employers/collaborators: My most polished and professional projects.
  • For lecturers/peers: Essay and blog posts explaining my design process.
  • For casual viewers: A mix of portfolio highlights, general blog updates, and the design process.

Interface Elements

My portfolio website includes a range of interactive elements that improve usability and align with my UI/UX goals. These features help users navigate smoothly, engage with content, and understand the structure of the site. Below is a summary of the key interface elements I implemented:

1. Buttons

Buttons are used throughout the site to support intuitive navigation and content exploration:

  • “Read More” / “View”: Found under blog entries, essays, and project previews to reveal full content.
  • “Back to Top”: Present at the bottom of longer pages, making it easier for users to return to the top.
  • “Back to Blog Page” / “Back to Essay Page”: Clearly directs users back to the overview page after viewing a specific post, replacing the need for breadcrumbs.

2. Sticky Navigation Bar

The navigation bar stays fixed at the top of the screen. On the homepage, it enables smooth scrolling to each section (e.g., Portfolio, Design Process). On other pages, it provides direct links to each main section of the site, ensuring easy access no matter where the user is.

3. Icons

I use icons as quick visual links to:

  • My GitHub profile
  • Contact options (e.g., email, Instagram)

These provide an efficient, minimal way for users to connect with me and explore more of my work.

4. Hover Effects

Buttons, links, and profile elements feature hover effects — such as color changes, movement, or animations — to signal interactivity. These small details enhance feedback and make the site feel more dynamic and polished.

5. Lightbox for Images and Animations

Portfolio items and animations use a lightbox feature that displays media in a fullscreen modal. This allows users to view images and videos without navigating away from the current page. Lightboxes also support keyboard navigation (arrows and ESC), improving both accessibility and user experience.

Style Process Documentation

My style process began with a clear intention: to design a website that feels clean, modern, and easy to navigate. From the start, I aimed for a minimalist visual aesthetic that focused on readability, a balance of text and imagery, and smooth user interaction. As the project developed, I refined and updated several elements—including colour palette, typography, and layout—to strengthen the overall design and improve user experience.

Visual Aesthetic and Layout

The overall aesthetic of the site is simple and uncluttered, supporting a smooth and focused browsing experience. I wanted the interface to feel modern and professional, with enough breathing space between elements to reduce cognitive load. A grid-based layout structure keeps everything aligned and consistent, while intentional spacing improves readability and guides the user’s attention to key content and navigation elements.

Colour Palette: Evolution and Refinement

Initially, I planned to use a neutral background (white or light beige) with accent colours like blue or green to highlight buttons and links. The idea was to keep the interface calm and organised, with subtle colour cues to help guide navigation.

Later, I refined the palette to make it more cohesive, accessible, and visually distinctive. I defined a system of primary, neutral, and secondary colours that provide stronger contrast, support content hierarchy, and add personality without overwhelming the design:

Primary Colours:

  • Black #000000 – For text, icons, and high-contrast UI elements.
  • Delft Blue #3C415B – A deep, refined tone used in headings, backgrounds, and core interface components.

Neutral Colours:

  • White #FFFFFF – Used to create clean visual contrast in large headers or page backgrounds.
  • Almond #E6DACE – A soft, warm neutral applied to cards and section dividers to visually group content.

Secondary Colours (Accents):

  • Ash Grey #B2C7B6 – A muted green used subtly for highlights, icons, and hover states.
  • Cambridge Blue #8DA69B – A soft accent used in borders or background details.
  • Jasper #B75B47 – A bold, warm tone used to draw attention to CTAs, numbers, or important text elements.

This refined system offers better balance, visual interest, and consistency across the site.

Typography: Initial Plan and Adjustments

Originally, I selected Poppins for headings and Georgia for body text. Poppins offered a modern, clean look that fit well with the design, while Georgia brought a classic, readable tone to longer paragraphs.

After further testing, I updated the body font to Tinos—a serif font like Georgia, but with better on-screen readability and a more modern feel. This combination of Poppins for headings and Tinos for body text now forms a strong typographic pairing that balances clarity, structure, and personality.

Font sizes follow a clear visual hierarchy to guide users through the page, distinguishing headings, subheadings, and paragraphs with consistent scaling and spacing.

Reference Material

  • The Seth Hampton portfolio website is my main inspiration. It can be found here. I like the layout of the site. Initially, I wanted my website to look like this, all on one page. However, in the end I found it to be a little much. What I don’t like about it is when you’re on a section such as “about” or “resume”, you have to scroll through the entire section to be able to proceed to the next section of the webpage.

  • I was also inspired by the Maya Nelson layout on Wix, which can be found here. I like the layout and design of the information in this example. It almost appears to be in the form of postcards that align in the centre of the page. What I don’t like, however, is the colour scheme. It’s a bit too bland. Initially, I thought it might be a somewhat soothing colour, but I want viewers to be energised and excited by my work, so I needed some colour that would be more active.
