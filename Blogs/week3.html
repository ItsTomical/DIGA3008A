User Alignment and Mapping Site Content, Experience, and Interface Elements

The Interaction Design slides describe alignment as having a clear goal, ensuring things are easy to do, and communicating well. The main goal in designing my website is to showcase my work in a simple, engaging manner. My focus will be on making the site easy to use and meeting the needs of different visitors (also known as “actors”). These will include casual viewers (such as family and friends), peers, lecturers/tutors, and potential employers or collaborators.

According to the slides, interaction happens when something responds to a user’s actions in a meaningful (not random) way, requires effort, and leads to a change. On my website, I want visitors to interact with my portfolio by clicking on a piece to see more details, context, and images. In the same way, on my blog and essay pages, users will see a heading and a short preview (or teaser) of each post. If they want to read more, they can click to access the full ßpost. My aim is for this design to encourage exploration, and I plan to link posts about my design process to guide my visitors toward related topics.

Another key point from the slides is that interfaces work as systems of signs, with a signifier (what you see) and a signified (its meaning or function). For example, a hyperlink’s visible text is the signifier, while its purpose (which is to take the user to another page) is the signified. I plan to apply this concept to my website by using portfolio thumbnails or description text as signifiers, leading my visitors to more information.

The slides also describe users as actors because they actively shape their experience through interaction. On my site, their actions – such as filtering portfolio pieces by category – will influence their journey, making navigation more personalised.

The slides highlight three key areas in interaction design: Information Architecture (IA), User Experience (UX), and User Interface (UI). IA is all about organising content so users can find what they need easily.

IA is about organising content so users can easily find what they are looking for. For my site, I plan to have six main sections: Home, Blogs, Essays, Portfolio, Design, and About, with a sticky navigation bar at the top for easy access. Everything will be structured in a way that makes sense and is usable. To help visitors find what they’re looking for, I will categorise my portfolio by project type and provide headings and short teasers for blog posts to allow them to scan through the topics. I will also link related content. By organising information clearly and providing links, I aim to make it easy for visitors to find what interests them.

UX is about making my website easy and enjoyable to use. I want visitors to smoothly move through the site, starting from the Home page, going to the About section to learn more about me, then exploring the work in my Portfolio, and finally checking out the Blogs and Essays for more insights.

UI is about how the website looks and how “actors” interact with it. As mentioned above, I plan to use a sticky navigation bar for easy access. I would also like to use breadcrumbs to show users where they are, and links to connect related content. Additionally, I would like to include buttons like “Read More” under blog previews and “View Portfolio” to guide users. On the Portfolio page, users will be able to filter projects by category to find relevant work. These elements will make the site visually appealing and easy to use.

Reflecting on Structure, URL Scheme, and the Role of Semantic Markup

One of the key points from the slides is that a well-organised site helps both users and systems. That's why I will set up my URLs to be clear and easy to follow. For example, my site will use simple URLs like:

  • DIGA3008A/home
  • DIGA3008A/blogs
  • Blogs/Week 1

This will help users to know where they are on the site. I also intend to use semantic HTML elements like < article >, < nav >, < section >, and < footer > to make the site more accessible and search-engine friendly. Using proper HTML tags will ensure my content is well-structured and meaningful. These elements will help improve navigation, boost search engine optimisation (SEO), and make the site easier to update in the future.
