Decolonialism: How Code Matters

23 May 2025

Reflecting on Verdi (2020) and Mohamed et al. (2020), this blog explores the ethical dimensions of web development through a decolonial lens. It considers how seemingly neutral design choices can reproduce systems of exclusion and highlights the tension between sleek interaction design and broad accessibility.

Decolonialism: How code matters

In building my website, I have come to realise that my coding decisions have deeper ethical implications, especially when viewed in the context of decolonialism. Verdi (2020) introduces the concept of techno-colonialism, where technology, particularly AI, is used by more powerful regions to maintain control over less powerful ones, creating systems of dependence. In the same way, my website could reflect power dynamics that benefit some users while leaving out others.

Mohamed et al. (2020) highlight that digital systems aren't as neutral as they might seem. They’re shaped by the values and assumptions of the people who create them, and this means they can unintentionally exclude certain groups. Most of the time, global technologies end up prioritising Western languages, cultural norms, and design choices, making them less accessible or relevant to users from other contexts. It’s something that even a simple website can reveal, especially when it assumes everyone has access to fast internet, the latest gadgets, or specific tech skills.

This got me thinking about my coding choices. I’ve added a few JavaScript features to my homepage that I think make the site easier to use and navigate. For example, the sticky navigation bar helps users jump easily between sections without reloading the page or having to scroll back to the top. I also included a carousel so people can view all my blog posts without leaving the homepage. And on the portfolio page, I set up a lightbox so clicking on an image brings it up in a larger format with context, without taking you away from the page. These changes are meant to make browsing my site feel more interactive and fun.

However, as Mohamed et al. (2020) point out, even well-intentioned design choices can create inequality. It made me think that by using a lot of JavaScript, I might be excluding people who have older devices, slow internet, or who need screen readers. This is part of what they call algorithmic colonialism, where digital systems embed power dynamics by privileging certain users while disadvantaging others. Verdi (2020) makes a similar point with data colonialism, where powerful regions extract data from others without giving much back. A website can do something similar if it assumes everyone has equal access to technology.

Thinking about this, I realise how the issues Mohamed et al. (2020) raise about power and bias in tech can show up in web design too. Just like how AI might seem neutral but actually reflects its creators’ biases, my website could be doing the same. By focusing on smooth animations and interactive features, I might make the site look great, but not everyone will be able to enjoy it. I need to remember who might get left behind.

References:
