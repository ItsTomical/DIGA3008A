Blog 13

An Ethical Internet

23 May 2025

What would an ethical internet look like? This blog reflects on fairness, access, privacy, and online behaviour, drawing on readings by Stein (2008), Papacharissi (2002), Floridi (2021), and Silverstone (Orgad, 2007).

An ethical internet

When I think of the internet, I picture this huge maze where you can find anything and everything, from helpful advice to the strangest content. Some areas are gated, some are full of ads, and some are just really unfriendly. So, what would an ethical internet look like to me? It would be a place that’s fair, safe, and open to everyone. But can that happen?

You might think that most of the online spaces we use are public. Wrong! Most are privately owned. This means our freedom to speak isn't guaranteed. Instead, as Stein (2008) says, it’s a privilege controlled by the platform owners. If they don’t like what we say, our posts can vanish or our accounts can be suspended. For me, an ethical internet means giving people spaces where they can speak their minds without constantly worrying about being shut down because their views aren’t popular.

Access and participation

Then there’s access. Zizi Papacharissi (2002) emphasises something really important: the internet is supposed to be this incredible space for public conversation, but not all of us can take part. If our connection is unstable, if we’re not skilled with technology, or if we’re scared to speak up, we will be left out. To me, an ethical internet isn’t just about having access though. It must give all of us, no matter who we are or how old we are, the confidence to take part. Digital skills shouldn’t be something only a few people have.

Trust and privacy

Privacy also matters. As Luciano Floridi (2021) notes, people want control over their personal information and need to trust it won’t be misused. Without that, they might avoid taking part, which, in my view, goes against the idea of a fair and open internet for everyone.

Kindness and accountability

But while things such as free speech are important, it doesn’t mean anything goes. Roger Silverstone (Orgad, 2007) describes the internet as a moral space, where how we treat each other matters just as much as what we’re allowed to say. We can all name toxic online spaces off the top of our head. X, for example, or Facebook, where people often say things in comments they’d never dare say to others face-to-face.

For me, an ethical internet strikes a balance between the freedom to express our opinions and taking responsibility for what we say. People should be able to share their views, but there also needs to be protection against harassment, hate speech, and other harmful behaviour. Cyberbullying, for example, is a serious problem that has even led some people to take their own lives.

For me, building a kinder, more respectful online world is something we need to do. It’s about ethics: treating each other with kindness and respect, even when we don’t agree.
