Designing with Purpose: Aligning Goals, Users, and Interface Elements

10 March 2025

In this post, I outline the goals of my personal website, explore different user types and their needs, and explain how interface elements, information structure, and visual design choices support a smooth and engaging user experience.

Goal Alignment

My goal

My goal for my website is to develop a platform where I can showcase my portfolio of work in a simple yet engaging manner, attracting a variety of users, including potential employers, collaborators and peers, as well as casual viewers, such as family and friends.

What I want users to see and do

I want users of my website to easily view and explore my work, giving insight into my creative process and providing an overview of my background and skill set. I want users to explore my portfolio and projects, and to read my blog posts and essays. The aim is to spark their interest in what I have to offer and for them to act by reaching out to me for feedback, employment or collaboration.

My User

My target audience for my website includes the different types of visitors listed below, all of which will have different needs:

  1. Potential employers and collaborators:

    Evaluate my skills and quality of work for potential work opportunities.

  2. Peers and lecturers:

    View my work for feedback, interest and academic purposes.

  3. Casual viewers:

    Friends or family, visiting out of interest and for an engaging experience.

My Content

The content of my website includes:

  1. Portfolio component:

    Visual displays of my works/projects, with descriptions.

  2. Blogs and essays:

    Reflections on design, interaction and media.

  3. Design component:

    A look at how I design and develop this website.

  4. About and contact component:

    Biography about my background, skills, and contact information.

What is the most important and interesting:

  • The most important content is what best represents my capabilities.
  • For employers/collaborators: My most polished and professional projects.
  • For lectures/peers: Essay and Blog posts explaining my design process.
  • For casual viewers: A mix of portfolio highlights, general Blog updates and the Design Process

Information Structure

User Flow

Interface Elements

Buttons:

I'll use buttons like “Read More” and “View” to guide visitors towards engaging with my content. These will appear on the homepage under the short descriptions for the blogs, essays, portfolio, and the design process. There will also be a “Back to Top” at the bottom of the blog and essay pages.

List Boxes:

On the blogs page, I'll implement a list box that allows users to select the specific blog they want to see. When they select a blog from the list, the page will scroll down to that particular post, giving them easy access to the content. Once they’ve found the post, they can click “Read More” to read the full blog.

Sticky Navigation Bar:

To ensure easy access to all sections of the website, I’ll use a sticky navigation bar that remains visible as the user scrolls.

Icons:

I'll use icons for quick visual references to links like my Contact information and Github.

Wireframes

My original wireframes have changed a lot in these second renditions. While the style and layout remain the same, the major change comes in here: I had originally thought of having everything on my home page, and the user clicks on what they want to view, and the page expands to open up the selected content. Now, when the user clicks on the content on the home page, it will take them to a new page (for example, the blogs page), which I believe makes for better flow and user experience.

Style Guide

Visual Aesthetic:

My website will have a simple, clean, and modern design. The focus will be on ensuring the content is easy to read and view, with a balance of text and images to improve the experience. The design will be easy to navigate, with no distractions, helping users to easily find what they need.

Colour Scheme:

I’ll use a simple colour scheme with neutral colours like white or light beige for the background, and accent colours like blue or green for buttons, links, and important words. This will help organise the page and make it easier for users to find their way.

Layout:

The layout will be neat and organised, using a grid system to keep everything in place. There will be enough space between elements to avoid clutter and make the site easy to read. Important features, like the navigation bar and buttons, will be easy to find.

Font Choices:

For headings, I’ll use Poppins, a modern, clean font that’s easy to read. For body text, I’ll use Georgia, a classic serif font that’s great for longer reading passages. The font sizes will follow a clear structure to guide users through the headings, subheadings, and body content.

Reflection

In terms of struggles, I found it frustrating that when committing and pushing a change, it takes a long time to appear on the website. I didn’t realise this and so I went back to edit the code because I thought something was wrong. However, I actually didn’t need to do this; I just needed to wait a bit longer.

Additionally, I have struggled with image placement, and will need to work on this. I need to get my website to a point where I am happy with the HTML and semantic markup before we start CSS and Java Script.

I am happy with and proud of the file structure on my site. I feel that all it needs is a bit of refinement as I progress through the development of the site.
