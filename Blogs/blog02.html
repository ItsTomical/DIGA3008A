Hypertext, Power, and the Internet We Have Today

24 February 2025

A reflection on Moulthrop’s and Nelson’s visions of a free, open internet — and how today’s web, shaped by algorithms, corporations, and governments, challenges the dream of true digital literacy and user control.

Moulthrop’s vision and the web we have today

Moulthrop (1993) suggests that hypertext wouldn’t replace books, but would change how we engage with media. This is true today – while books continue to exist, the internet involves much more interactivity than mere reading. Moulthrop (1993) reflects on Nelson’s vision of hypertext, and his system called Xanadu. Nelson saw hypertext as a “return of literacy” (Moulthrop, 1993, p.695), a way for people to explore ideas beyond books and create information instead of just reading it.

He believed literacy was only for the elite, limiting access to knowledge. His vision for Xanadu was of a network that allowed users to “manipulate and alter the textual corpus” (Moulthrop, 1993, p.692); where they could control the information themselves rather than leaving it to companies or governments to decide what gets shared.

Corporate and political control

Moulthrop (1993) warns that hypertext could still be taken over by gatekeeping forces. This is exactly what we see today, with the web largely controlled by a few big tech companies. Most people use platforms like Facebook, X or Google to get their news, but what they find is information shaped by algorithms that determine what they see.

Also, think of the political control in China, where citizens’ access is restricted by the Great Firewall, which blocks sites like Google, Wikipedia and many more.

Is the web really participatory?

Instead of the open web that Nelson imagined, companies and governments today control what information we see online. While the internet lets us create videos, share opinions, and post blogs (like this one), participation is still limited and controlled. Social media companies decide what content gets seen and what gets ignored.

Even though people create content, businesses and governments still control what is shared and what is hidden to suit their interests.

Secondary literacy and the missing layer of understanding

Moulthrop (1993) also talks about “secondary literacy”, where people don’t just use hypertext, but actually understand how it works. Nelson saw hypertext as a way to get more people involved with information so that they could think more critically.

This is not the case today. It is true that the internet has made it easier to create and share content, but not everyone knows how it works. Most of us just consume content and don’t have the power to shape it or control what we see. So, we end up following what companies decide we should see.

Conclusion

Moulthrop’s (1993) idea of a hypertext revolution hasn’t fully come to life. While the internet has made communication more interactive, its potential is held back by corporate and political control, algorithms, and unequal access to digital skills. Nelson’s dream of Xanadu as a free and open system for sharing information hasn’t come true.

So we have to ask the question, can the internet still become the knowledge-sharing place Nelson had hoped for, or has it become just another tool for those in power?
