Blog 9

Design Justice, Digital Access, and Ethical Tech

21 April 2025

A critical response to Daniolou, Costanza-Chock, and Cantrill that argues internet access is a basic right—and that ethical, inclusive design must centre the voices of those most affected by digital exclusion.

Access to the Internet as a Basic Right

Access to the internet is no longer something extra; it’s something people need to live and work in today’s world. In her article, The Need for Global Internet Connectivity, Claire Daniolou explains that not having internet can lead to being left out socially, having less opportunities to earn money, and struggling to get education and healthcare, especially in rural areas. I agree that internet access should be treated as a basic right. Without it, people are cut off from many of the things that others rely on every day. But people also need to know how to use the internet in useful and safe ways. To close the digital divide, we need to invest in building infrastructure and also in teaching digital skills. If we want a more equal and connected world, we have to make sure no one is left behind.

Design Justice and Inclusive Design

This idea of including everyone links closely to the concept of design justice. Sasha Costanza-Chock writes in Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the World We Need that design is often seen as a way to fix problems or make things more efficient. But design is not neutral; it is shaped by power and affects who gets included and who is left out. Design justice recognises that people with more privilege and influence often control how things are designed, which can lead to unfair results. Design choices, whether on a digital platform or in a physical space, can increase inequality by limiting access for some groups.

If we want a fairer society, we have to look at who holds the power in the design process and try to change that. Design justice tells us that people who are most affected by a problem should be the ones helping to create the solution. This ensures the design process is fair, inclusive, and meets the needs of everyone, not just those who already have power. I agree with this approach. When communities are left out of the design process, the result is systems that don’t meet their needs or even make things worse. To build a society that is fair, we need to share the power in design and include the people most affected so that the process works for everyone and they can trust it.

Ethical Design and Community-Led Projects

I support how design justice values local, community-led projects. The tech world is obsessed with growing things fast and reaching the biggest audience possible. But small and slow can be better, especially when it means building trust and creating something that actually works for people. This connects to Bryan Cantrill’s talk on ethical dilemmas in software. He points out that engineers are shaping society but don’t always think about ethics. That’s why design justice matters. It puts fairness and accountability at the centre of the process. Ethical design means thinking about what’s right, not just what’s possible. It should work well and also respect the needs of the people using it.

In my opinion, design justice is the direction we need to go. If we want more inclusive and ethical systems, we have to design with and not just for the people most affected.
