Blog 11

Understanding Digital Inequality

05 May 2025

Reflecting on Robinson et al. (2015), this blog explores how digital inequality goes beyond access, affecting how people engage with technology across age, gender, and geography. It argues that true digital inclusion requires not just devices, but confidence, skills, and meaningful support.

Understanding digital inequality

In this week’s reading, Digital Inequalities and Why They Matter, Robinson et al. (2015) explain that digital inequality isn’t just about who has a computer or internet access. It’s also about how people use technology, whether they feel comfortable with it, and what they can actually do online. They show that digital inequality is tied to other forms of inequality, like age, gender, race and income, and can make those problems even worse.

Capability matters

Robinson et al. (2015) tell us that having internet access isn’t enough; what really matters is knowing how to use it and feeling at ease in engaging with it. It made me think of when I’ve seen people struggle, not because they didn’t own a phone or a laptop, but because they weren’t sure how to use them or were hesitant to ask for help.

Age and the digital divide

A point they raise is how digital inequality affects people of different ages differently. For example, some school students might have to share a device at home and so have little time online, “often depriving them of opportunities to develop the kinds of valuable web skills which their less constrained peers enjoy” (Robinson et al., 2015, p.571). It got me thinking about how, in some homes, children have to take turns using one device, which means less time for classes, assignments, or even just asking for help when they’re stuck. Meanwhile, older adults might avoid using technology altogether because they find it confusing. I’ve seen this in real life. For older people, even basic apps can be overwhelming without support. My 86-year-old gran, for example, often asks me to help her sort out the apps on her phone because she finds them confusing. This shows how something as everyday as using a smartphone can be difficult if you’re not confident or haven’t had much experience with digital tools.

The digital divide also shows up in the lives of middle-aged adults, who Robinson et al. (2015) say often care for both children and elderly parents. As they put it, “for those who can make effective use of digital resources during this life stage, caretaking burdens can be eased” (Robinson et al., 2015, p.572). This reminds me of my neighbour, who set up a digital camera to monitor her elderly mother who lived thousands of kilometres away. When her mother fell and broke her hip, the camera’s two-way audio allowed my neighbour to speak to her and call for help quickly. In that moment, the camera wasn’t just a tool; it was what made it possible for her to act fast and prevent further harm.

Stories like this show how powerful digital tools can be when people have access to them and know how to use them. But they also make it clear how tough things can be for people who don’t have the same resources. If you don’t have the finances, don’t know where to start with technology, or aren’t comfortable using it, it’s much harder to care for someone from a distance or to just stay connected. I wonder then, if these digital tools can ease burdens and even save lives, shouldn’t governments step in to make sure everyone has the necessary access, training and support?

Gender and technology

Digital inequality doesn’t just show up in who has access or how old someone is; it also shows up in how gender plays out online. One example that stuck with me from the reading was a study where people created avatars in virtual environments and often exaggerated traditional gender traits. Robinson et al. (2015, p.573) mention that “the gender stereotyping present in the offline world can appear in exaggerated forms in online environments”. It struck me how male characters are often made to look tough or in control, while female ones come across as softer or more passive. We like to think of digital spaces as more open or progressive, but they can end up repeating the same old gender stereotypes we see offline.

So, are these digital spaces really as liberating as we think? Without thoughtful design and awareness, they can easily end up repeating old patterns. Reading this made me realise how important it is to build online environments that actually support equality, where people aren’t boxed in by outdated gender roles, but feel free to be themselves.

A global perspective on digital inequality

This reading really made me think about how global digital inequality is. We often hear that the internet is “borderless”, but that’s not the reality for everyone. In many places, especially across parts of Africa, whole communities are still offline. Robinson et al. (2015) point out that “from a global perspective, digital inequalities often augment racial and ethnic disparities... in the case of Africa, global digital inequalities have reinforced existing racial as well as economic chasms, shutting out a huge proportion of the continent from access to the internet.” In some homes, children don’t even have their own device, which makes it nearly impossible to keep up in a world that assumes everyone is always connected. This makes me wonder how many students fell behind during the COVID-19 pandemic because they didn’t have the necessary technology. The pandemic highlighted how digital access is tied to education, work, healthcare, and even basic connection. Those without the right tools or skills were left behind.

What stood out to me most, though, was the reminder that digital exclusion isn’t just a problem in poor or remote places. Robinson et al. (2015) show that it exists in wealthier countries too, just in different ways. I’ve started noticing it more: people who have devices but don’t feel confident using them, or who avoid certain tasks because they’re too confusing or frustrating.

At the heart of it, for me

What really struck me in this reading is how it calls on all of us to care about digital inequality. Today, so much of our lives happen online. When you are digitally excluded, you’re not just missing out on the latest technology; you’re being left out of essential parts of society, like work, education, and social connections. This reading helped me to see that digital inequality is more than a technology issue; it’s a social issue with real consequences for people’s everyday lives.

It's also made me aware of the hidden barriers people face online. This is not just about owning a device or being online. It’s about having confidence and skills to engage meaningfully with technology. Solving digital inequality then isn’t just about getting people online; it’s about ensuring we all have the support and tools to fully participate in the digital world.

References:
